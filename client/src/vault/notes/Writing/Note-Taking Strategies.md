# Note-Taking Strategies

Note-taking is not transcription. The goal is not to record what was said or written — it is to *process* it into a form that supports future thinking. The best note-taking strategies are those that force active engagement with the material.

## Why Most Note-Taking Fails

Most people take notes by highlighting or copying. This feels productive but produces no learning. The *generation effect* in cognitive psychology shows that information is better retained when it is *generated* (recalled, rewritten, explained) than when it is merely *read*.

> "The best way to learn is to teach." — Richard Feynman

## The Cornell Method

Developed at Cornell University in the 1950s, the Cornell method divides the page into three sections:

```
┌─────────────────────┬──────────────────────┐
│   Cue column        │   Note-taking column  │
│   (keywords,        │   (main notes during  │
│   questions)        │   lecture/reading)    │
│                     │                       │
│                     │                       │
├─────────────────────┴──────────────────────┤
│   Summary (written after, in your own words)│
└─────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The cue column is filled *after* taking notes, by writing questions or keywords that prompt recall. The summary is written from memory, without looking at the notes.

## The Feynman Technique

1. Choose a concept.
2. Explain it in plain language, as if teaching a child.
3. Identify gaps — where did you get stuck or vague?
4. Return to the source to fill the gaps.
5. Simplify further.

The Feynman Technique is a diagnostic tool: it reveals what you actually understand versus what you merely recognise.

## Progressive Summarisation (Tiago Forte)

Progressive summarisation is a method for distilling notes over multiple passes:

| Pass | Action |
|------|--------|
| Layer 1 | Save the source (article, highlight, quote) |
| Layer 2 | Bold the most important passages |
| Layer 3 | Highlight the most important bold passages |
| Layer 4 | Write an executive summary in your own words |
| Layer 5 | Remix into a new note or piece of writing |

The key insight: you do not need to distil everything at once. Distil a little more each time you return to a note.

## Evergreen Notes (Andy Matuschak)

Andy Matuschak's concept of **evergreen notes** is a refinement of the [[The Zettelkasten Method|Zettelkasten]] permanent note:

- Written in complete sentences (not bullets or fragments)
- About a single concept (atomic)
- Titled as an assertion, not a topic: "Evergreen notes should be atomic" rather than "Evergreen notes"
- Densely linked to other notes
- Revised and updated over time (hence "evergreen")

The title-as-assertion is the key innovation: it forces you to commit to a claim, which makes the note easier to link and easier to use in writing.

## Choosing a Strategy

No single strategy is best for all contexts:

| Context | Recommended Strategy |
|---------|---------------------|
| Lectures / talks | Cornell method |
| Books / articles | Progressive summarisation |
| Learning a concept | Feynman technique |
| Building a knowledge base | Zettelkasten / evergreen notes |
| Quick capture | Fleeting notes → process later |

The best system is the one you will actually use. Start simple and add complexity only when you feel the friction of the simpler system.
