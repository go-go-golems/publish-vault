# Stoicism

Stoicism is a school of Hellenistic philosophy founded by [[Zeno of Citium]] in Athens around 300 BC. It teaches that virtue is the only true good, that external events are indifferent, and that the path to *eudaimonia* — flourishing — lies in aligning one's will with reason and nature.

## The Dichotomy of Control

The most practically useful Stoic teaching is the **dichotomy of control**, articulated most clearly by Epictetus in the *Enchiridion*:

> "Some things are in our control and others not. Things in our control are opinion, pursuit, desire, aversion, and, in a word, whatever are our own actions. Things not in our control are body, reputation, command, and, in one word, whatever are not our own actions."
> — Epictetus, *Enchiridion* §1

This distinction is not passive resignation. It is a precise allocation of attention: invest effort only where effort can make a difference.

## The Four Virtues

The Stoics held that virtue (*aretê*) is the only genuine good. Everything else — health, wealth, reputation — is *preferred indifferent* (*proêgmena adiaphora*): worth pursuing when available, but not worth compromising virtue to obtain.

| Virtue | Greek | Description |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| Wisdom | *Phronêsis* | Knowing what is truly good, bad, and indifferent |
| Courage | *Andreia* | Facing difficulty without being governed by fear |
| Justice | *Dikaiosynê* | Treating all rational beings fairly |
| Temperance | *Sôphrosynê* | Moderation; not being enslaved to appetite |

## Key Figures

The Stoic tradition spans six centuries and three continents.

### The Early Stoa (Athens)
- **[[Zeno of Citium]]** (c. 334–262 BC) — founder; taught in the *Stoa Poikile*
- **Cleanthes** (c. 330–230 BC) — second head of the school; author of the *Hymn to Zeus*
- **Chrysippus** (c. 279–206 BC) — systematised Stoic logic and physics

### The Middle Stoa
- **Panaetius** (c. 185–110 BC) — brought Stoicism to Rome
- **Posidonius** (c. 135–51 BC) — polymath; influenced Cicero

### The Late Stoa (Rome)
- **Seneca** (c. 4 BC–65 AD) — statesman and playwright; *Letters to Lucilius*
- **Epictetus** (c. 50–135 AD) — former slave; *Discourses* and *Enchiridion*
- **Marcus Aurelius** (121–180 AD) — Roman emperor; *Meditations*

## Stoic Physics and Cosmology

The Stoics were materialists. They held that reality consists of two principles: *logos* (active, rational principle, identified with God or Zeus) and *hylê* (passive matter). The universe is a single living organism pervaded by *pneuma* — a fiery breath that gives it coherence and rationality.

This cosmology supports the ethical teaching: if the universe is rational and good, then aligning oneself with its logos is both natural and virtuous.

## A Practical Exercise: The Negative Visualisation

One Stoic practice is *premeditatio malorum* — the premeditation of adversity:

```
Morning reflection:
  1. Identify one thing you value today (health, a relationship, your work).
  2. Spend two minutes vividly imagining its loss.
  3. Notice the gratitude that arises.
  4. Proceed with the day, holding the thing lightly.
```

This is not pessimism. It is inoculation against the hedonic adaptation that makes us take good things for granted.

## Relationship to Other Schools

Stoicism shares the goal of *eudaimonia* with Aristotle's virtue ethics, but disagrees sharply on whether external goods are necessary for flourishing. It differs from [[Epistemology|Epicureanism]] (often confused with it) in viewing pleasure as an indifferent rather than a good.
