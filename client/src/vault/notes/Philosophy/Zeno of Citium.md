# Zeno of Citium

Zeno of Citium (c. 334–262 BC) was a Hellenistic philosopher from Citium (modern Larnaca, Cyprus). He is best known as the founder of [[Stoicism]], which he established in Athens around 300 BC.

## Life and Background

Zeno came from a Phoenician merchant family. According to tradition, he was shipwrecked near Athens while transporting a cargo of purple dye from Phoenicia. Stranded and penniless, he wandered into a bookshop and read Xenophon's *Memorabilia* — an account of Socrates. Captivated, he asked the bookseller where he might find such a man. The bookseller pointed to the Cynic philosopher Crates of Thebes, who happened to be passing by.

> "I made a prosperous voyage when I suffered shipwreck." — Zeno, on his misfortune

## Philosophical Development

Zeno studied under several philosophers before founding his own school:

1. **Crates of Thebes** — Cynic philosopher; taught Zeno to disregard social convention
2. **Stilpo of Megara** — Megarian logician; influenced Zeno's logic
3. **Xenocrates** and **Polemo** — heads of Plato's Academy; shaped Zeno's ethics

From the Cynics he took the idea that virtue is sufficient for happiness. From the Megarians he took an interest in logic. From the Academy he took the framework of systematic philosophy. [[Stoicism]] synthesised these influences into a unified system.

## The Stoa Poikile

Zeno taught in the *Stoa Poikile* — the "Painted Porch" — a covered walkway in the Athenian agora decorated with murals of the Battle of Marathon. His followers became known as *Stoikoi* ("people of the porch"), and the school as Stoicism.

## Legacy

Zeno's own writings survive only in fragments quoted by later authors. His legacy was preserved and extended by his successors, especially Chrysippus, who systematised Stoic logic and physics so thoroughly that it was said: "If there had been no Chrysippus, there would have been no Stoa."

The school Zeno founded endured for six centuries, producing [[Stoicism|Stoic]] thinkers from Athens to Rome, from freedmen like Epictetus to emperors like Marcus Aurelius.
