# Epistemology

Epistemology is the branch of philosophy that studies the nature, scope, and limits of human knowledge. Its central questions are deceptively simple: *What is knowledge? How is it acquired? What can we know, and what must remain uncertain?*

## The Classical Definition: Justified True Belief

The standard starting point is Plato's analysis in the *Meno* and *Theaetetus*. A person S **knows** that P if and only if:

1. **P is true** — you cannot know a falsehood
2. **S believes that P** — knowledge requires mental assent
3. **S is justified in believing P** — the belief must be grounded, not lucky

This is the **Justified True Belief** (JTB) account. It dominated epistemology for two millennia.

## The Gettier Problem

In 1963, Edmund Gettier published a three-page paper that shook the field. He constructed cases where all three JTB conditions are satisfied, yet we intuitively feel the person does *not* know.

**Gettier's Example (paraphrased):**

> Smith has strong evidence that Jones will get the job, and Jones has ten coins in his pocket. Smith concludes: "The man who will get the job has ten coins in his pocket." In fact, Smith gets the job — and Smith himself happens to have ten coins in his pocket. Smith's belief is true and justified, but it seems wrong to say he *knew* it.

The Gettier problem launched fifty years of attempts to patch the JTB account by adding a fourth condition.

## Responses to Gettier

| Approach | Key Idea | Proponent |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| No false lemmas | Knowledge cannot rest on any false intermediate step | Harman (1973) |
| Causal theory | The belief must be causally connected to the fact | Goldman (1967) |
| Reliabilism | The belief must be produced by a reliable process | Goldman (1979) |
| Virtue epistemology | Knowledge requires intellectual virtue | Sosa, Zagzebski |
| Knowledge-first | Knowledge is primitive; don't analyse it into components | Williamson (2000) |

## A Priori vs A Posteriori

A fundamental distinction in epistemology concerns the *source* of knowledge:

- **A priori** knowledge is independent of experience. Mathematical truths (`2 + 2 = 4`) and logical tautologies (`all bachelors are unmarried`) are known purely by reason.
- **A posteriori** (empirical) knowledge depends on sensory experience. That water boils at 100°C at sea level is known by observation.

Kant complicated this picture by arguing that some synthetic (non-tautological) truths are known a priori — for example, that every event has a cause.

## Scepticism

Sceptical arguments challenge the possibility of knowledge altogether.

### Cartesian Scepticism

Descartes asked: how do I know I am not dreaming? Or that an evil demon is not deceiving me about everything? These scenarios are compatible with all my experiences yet incompatible with knowledge of the external world.

### The Brain in a Vat

A modern variant: suppose your brain has been removed and placed in a vat of nutrients, with electrodes feeding it simulated experiences. How could you tell? If you cannot rule this out, how can you claim to know anything about the external world?

```
Sceptical argument structure:
  P1: If S knows that P, S can rule out all alternatives to P.
  P2: S cannot rule out the brain-in-a-vat scenario.
  P3: The brain-in-a-vat scenario is an alternative to P.
  ∴   S does not know that P.
```

Responses include **contextualism** (knowledge attributions are context-sensitive), **relevant alternatives** (only nearby alternatives need be ruled out), and **dogmatism** (some beliefs are properly basic).
