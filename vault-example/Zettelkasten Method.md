# The Zettelkasten Method

The Zettelkasten (German: "slip box") is a knowledge management system developed by the sociologist **Niklas Luhmann** (1927–1998). Luhmann used it to write over 70 books and 400 scholarly articles.

## Core Principles

### 1. Atomic Notes

Each note should contain **one idea only**. This forces clarity of thought and makes notes reusable across different contexts.

### 2. Unique Identifiers

Every note gets a unique ID. Luhmann used alphanumeric IDs (e.g., `21/3a1b`). In digital systems, slugs or timestamps work well.

### 3. Linking

Notes are connected through explicit links. This is where the value emerges — a web of interconnected ideas that can generate new insights.

### 4. The Slip Box as a Conversation Partner

> "I only do what is easy. I only write when I immediately know how to do it. If I falter for a moment, I put the matter aside and do something else." — Niklas Luhmann

## Types of Notes

1. **Fleeting notes** — quick captures, to be processed later
2. **Literature notes** — summaries of what you read
3. **Permanent notes** — atomic ideas in your own words
4. **Index notes** — entry points into clusters of ideas (like [[Index]])

## Relationship to Philosophy

The Zettelkasten method has deep connections to [[Epistemology]] — it is fundamentally about how we acquire, organise, and extend knowledge. The method embodies the [[Stoicism|Stoic]] principle of careful, systematic reasoning.
