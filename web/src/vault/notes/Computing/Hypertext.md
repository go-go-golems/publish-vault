# Hypertext

Hypertext is text that contains links to other texts. It is the foundational concept of the World Wide Web, of wikis, of Obsidian, and of this very document you are reading.

The word was coined by **Ted Nelson** in 1963, but the idea is older.

## Vannevar Bush and the Memex (1945)

The earliest clear articulation of hypertext appeared in Vannevar Bush's 1945 essay *As We May Think*, published in *The Atlantic*. Bush imagined a device called the **Memex**:

> "A memex is a device in which an individual stores all his books, records, and communications, and which is mechanised so that it may be consulted with exceeding speed and flexibility. It is an enlarged intimate supplement to his memory."

The Memex would allow users to create *trails* — sequences of linked documents — that could be saved and shared. Bush's insight was that human thought is *associative*, not hierarchical. The Memex would mirror this structure.

The Memex was never built. But it inspired everyone who came after.

## Ted Nelson and Xanadu (1960s–present)

Ted Nelson coined the word **hypertext** in 1963 and spent the next sixty years building (and never finishing) a system called **Xanadu** — a global hypertext repository with bidirectional links, transclusion, and version history.

Nelson's vision was more radical than the Web:

- **Bidirectional links** — every link would be visible from both ends
- **Transclusion** — content could be included by reference, not copied
- **Micropayments** — authors would be compensated when their content was read

The Web, when it arrived, implemented a simpler version: unidirectional links, no transclusion, no payments. Nelson considered this a tragic simplification.

## Douglas Engelbart and NLS (1968)

In 1968, Douglas Engelbart gave what became known as the **Mother of All Demos** — a live demonstration of NLS (oN-Line System) that showed, for the first time:

- Hypertext links between documents
- The computer mouse
- Video conferencing
- Collaborative real-time editing
- A windowed interface

Everything in the modern personal computer was demonstrated in that ninety-minute session. The audience was stunned.

## Tim Berners-Lee and the Web (1989–1991)

Tim Berners-Lee, working at CERN, proposed a hypertext system for sharing documents among physicists in 1989. His proposal was titled *Information Management: A Proposal* and was returned by his manager with the annotation: "Vague but exciting."

By 1991, the World Wide Web was live. Berners-Lee's key decisions:

- **HTML** — a simple markup language for documents
- **HTTP** — a simple protocol for transferring them
- **URLs** — a universal addressing scheme
- **Open standard** — no patents, no licensing fees

The simplicity was the point. A more powerful system (like Xanadu) would have taken longer to build and been harder to adopt.

## Hypertext and the Zettelkasten

The [[The Zettelkasten Method|Zettelkasten]] is a physical hypertext system. Luhmann's index cards are nodes; his references are links. The insight that knowledge is a *network*, not a hierarchy, is the same insight that motivated Bush, Nelson, and Berners-Lee.

Modern tools like Obsidian are the convergence of these two traditions: the personal knowledge management of the Zettelkasten and the linking infrastructure of hypertext.
