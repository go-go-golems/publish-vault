# How to Write Clearly

Clear writing is not a gift. It is a skill, and like all skills it is learned through practice and feedback. The good news: the principles are simple. The hard news: applying them consistently requires constant vigilance.

> "The most valuable of all talents is that of never using two words when one will do." — Thomas Jefferson

## The Core Principle: One Idea Per Sentence

Most unclear writing fails at the sentence level. Writers pack too many ideas into a single sentence, forcing the reader to hold multiple things in mind simultaneously.

**Unclear:**
> The fact that the system, which had been designed by a committee of engineers who had varying levels of experience with distributed systems and who were working under significant time pressure due to the impending product launch, failed to account for network partitions, which are a well-known failure mode in distributed systems, was a significant contributing factor to the outage.

**Clear:**
> The system failed to account for network partitions. This is a well-known failure mode in distributed systems. The design team was under time pressure, which may explain the oversight.

The revision uses three sentences instead of one. Each sentence contains one idea. The reader can process them in sequence.

## Seven Rules

### 1. Prefer the active voice

Active: *The committee approved the proposal.*
Passive: *The proposal was approved by the committee.*

The active voice is shorter, clearer, and more direct. Use the passive only when the actor is unknown or unimportant.

### 2. Use strong verbs

Weak: *The system made a failure.*
Strong: *The system failed.*

Nominalisation — turning verbs into nouns — is the most common source of bureaucratic prose. Restore the verb.

### 3. Cut every unnecessary word

Wordy: *Due to the fact that the server was down, we were unable to process the request.*
Tight: *Because the server was down, we could not process the request.*

George Orwell's rule: "If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out."

### 4. Put the main idea at the start

Readers decide whether to keep reading based on the first sentence. Put the most important information first, then elaborate.

### 5. Use concrete nouns and specific details

Abstract: *The system experienced performance degradation.*
Concrete: *The API took 12 seconds to respond instead of 200 milliseconds.*

Numbers, names, and examples are always more persuasive than abstractions.

### 6. Vary sentence length

A sequence of short sentences feels choppy. A sequence of long sentences feels exhausting. Alternate between them. Short sentences create emphasis. Longer sentences allow you to develop an idea, show its connections to other ideas, and give the reader time to think.

### 7. Read it aloud

If you stumble while reading your own writing aloud, the reader will stumble too. Rewrite until it flows.

## The Revision Process

First drafts are not meant to be clear. They are meant to exist. Clarity comes in revision.

```
Draft 1: Get the ideas down. Do not edit.
Draft 2: Restructure. Does the argument flow?
Draft 3: Cut. Remove every word that does not earn its place.
Draft 4: Polish. Fix sentence rhythm, word choice, transitions.
Draft 5: Read aloud. Fix anything that sounds wrong.
```
