# The Writing Process

Writing is not a single act. It is a sequence of distinct cognitive modes, each requiring different mental states and different tools. Conflating them — trying to draft and edit simultaneously — is the most common source of writer's block.

## The Modes

### 1. Generating (Divergent)

In the generating phase, the goal is *quantity*, not quality. You are exploring the space of possible ideas. No idea is too stupid to write down. Editing is forbidden.

This is the phase where the [[The Zettelkasten Method|Zettelkasten]] pays off. If you have been taking notes, you are not starting from a blank page — you are assembling from a warehouse.

**Techniques:**
- Freewriting (write without stopping for 10–20 minutes)
- Brain dump (list every idea related to the topic)
- Talking (explain the topic to someone; record it)

### 2. Structuring (Analytical)

Once you have raw material, you need to find the argument. What is the *one thing* you are trying to say? Everything else is either supporting evidence or a distraction.

**Questions to ask:**
- What is my thesis? (One sentence.)
- What are the three strongest pieces of evidence for it?
- What are the strongest objections? How do I answer them?
- What does the reader need to know first?

### 3. Drafting (Generative + Analytical)

Drafting is the act of writing complete sentences and paragraphs. The goal is to get a complete draft, however rough. Do not stop to fix sentences. Do not look things up. Leave placeholders (`[CITATION]`, `[CHECK THIS]`) and keep moving.

> "The first draft of anything is shit." — Ernest Hemingway

### 4. Revising (Critical)

Revision is where writing becomes good. It is fundamentally different from drafting — it requires distance and ruthlessness.

**Revision checklist:**
- [ ] Does the opening sentence make the reader want to continue?
- [ ] Is the thesis clear by the end of the first paragraph?
- [ ] Does each paragraph have a clear topic sentence?
- [ ] Does each sentence follow logically from the previous one?
- [ ] Is every word earning its place?
- [ ] Are there any passive constructions that should be active?
- [ ] Are there any nominalisations that should be verbs?

See [[How to Write Clearly]] for sentence-level revision techniques.

### 5. Editing (Technical)

Editing is the final pass: grammar, punctuation, spelling, formatting. This is the phase where you fix the small things. Do it last, not first.

### 6. Publishing

Publishing is the act of releasing the work. For notes in a Zettelkasten, this might mean marking a note as "permanent". For an article, it means hitting send. The key is that publishing is a *decision*, not a default.

## The Iterative Nature of Writing

The phases above are not strictly sequential. You will often need to return to an earlier phase:

```
Generate → Structure → Draft
                ↑           ↓
           (restructure) Revise
                ↑           ↓
           (regenerate) Edit → Publish
```

This is normal. The goal is not to avoid iteration but to do each phase *consciously* — to know which mode you are in and to resist the temptation to switch prematurely.

## The Role of Note-Taking

A mature [[Personal Knowledge Management|PKM]] system transforms the writing process. The generating phase becomes assembly rather than invention. The structuring phase becomes selection rather than creation. The drafting phase becomes arrangement rather than discovery.

This is the promise of [[The Zettelkasten Method]]: writing becomes the *output* of thinking, not the *site* of thinking.
