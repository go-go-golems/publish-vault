# The Examined Life

> "The unexamined life is not worth living." — Socrates, *Apology* 38a

This is perhaps the most famous sentence in Western philosophy. Socrates spoke it at his trial in 399 BC, moments before the jury voted to execute him. He was offered the chance to propose a lesser punishment — exile, perhaps — but instead suggested that the city pay him a salary to continue philosophising. The jury voted for death.

## What Socrates Meant

The statement is not a counsel of perfectionism or self-obsession. Socrates was not recommending endless introspection. He was making a claim about the *kind of life* that is distinctively human.

Animals live. Plants live. What distinguishes human existence is the capacity for *logos* — reason, language, argument. To live without exercising this capacity — to drift through life on habit, appetite, and social convention — is to fail to be fully human.

The examined life is the life in which one:

- Asks what one's values actually are (not what one assumes they are)
- Tests those values against argument and experience
- Revises them when they prove inconsistent or unfounded
- Acts in accordance with the values that survive scrutiny

## The Socratic Method

Socrates' technique was *elenchus* — refutation through questioning. He would approach someone reputed to be wise — a politician, a poet, a craftsman — and ask them to define a virtue: *What is courage? What is piety? What is justice?*

The interlocutor would offer a definition. Socrates would find a counterexample. The interlocutor would refine the definition. Socrates would find another counterexample. This would continue until the interlocutor admitted they did not know what they thought they knew.

```
Socratic dialogue structure:
  1. Claim: "I know what X is."
  2. Definition: "X is Y."
  3. Counterexample: "But what about case Z, which is X but not Y?"
  4. Revised definition: "X is Y, except when Z."
  5. Repeat until aporia (puzzlement).
```

The goal is not to leave the interlocutor in despair but to replace *false* confidence with *genuine* inquiry.

## Aporia as a Starting Point

The state of *aporia* — being at a loss, not knowing where to turn — is not a failure. For Socrates, it is the necessary precondition for genuine learning. You cannot fill a cup that is already full.

This connects to [[Epistemology]]: the Socratic tradition is one of the earliest sustained engagements with the question of what we can know and how we know it.

## Relationship to Stoicism

[[Stoicism]] inherited the Socratic commitment to self-examination. The Stoics made it a daily practice: Marcus Aurelius wrote his *Meditations* as a private journal of self-correction. Epictetus taught that the first task of philosophy is to examine one's own impressions before acting on them.

## A Daily Practice

The examined life does not require a philosophy degree. It requires a habit:

- [ ] At the end of each day, ask: *What did I do well? What could I have done better?*
- [ ] When you feel a strong emotion, ask: *What belief is driving this feeling?*
- [ ] When you are about to act, ask: *Is this consistent with what I actually value?*
- [ ] Once a week, read something that challenges your current views.
